* **Year:** 2015 (Received: November 27, 2014; Published: February 13, 2015)
* **Title:** Gravitational Lensing by Spinning Black Holes in Astrophysics, and in the Movie *Interstellar*
* **Venue:** *Classical and Quantum Gravity*, Volume 32, Number 6, 065001

## 2. Core Contribution
The paper introduces **DNGR** (Double Negative Gravitational Renderer), an offline relativistic rendering framework designed to eliminate spatial flickering, aliasing, and moiré patterns in high-resolution (IMAX-quality) visualizations of Kerr black holes and accretion disks. 

Unlike conventional astrophysical codes that trace individual, zero-width light rays (which require high-frequency Monte Carlo supersampling to avoid aliasing), DNGR’s core contribution is the integration of **finite-width ray bundles (light beams)**. By integrating the **equations of geodesic deviation** (equivalent to the Sachs optical scalar equations) backward from the camera to the light source, the renderer maps each pixel to an explicit ellipse on the celestial sphere or accretion disk. This enables analytic spatial filtering (using a truncated Gaussian profile) and analytic motion blur (by calculating the time-derivatives of the swept beam).

## 3. Key Equations & Quantities

### Coordinates and Units
* **Spacetime Metric:** Boyer-Lindquist coordinates $(t, r, \theta, \phi)$.
* **Units:** Geometric units where $G = M = c = 1$. The black hole mass $M$ is set to 1, meaning all distances are expressed in units of $M$.
* **Spin Parameter:** $a$ (dimensionless spin parameter $a/M$, with near-extremal spin evaluated at $a = 0.999$, and a visually scaled down model at $a = 0.6$).

### Camera and Observer Reference Frames
* **Fiducial Observer (FIDO):** Locally non-rotating observer orthogonal to slices of constant $t$. The FIDO’s orthonormal basis vectors lie along coordinate lines: $\{e_{\hat{r}}, e_{\hat{\theta}}, e_{\hat{\phi}}\}$.
* **Camera Coordinate Location:** $(r_c, \theta_c, \phi_c)$.
* **Camera Velocity:** Speed $\beta$ and direction unit vector $B$ relative to the local FIDO.
* **Camera Orthonormal Basis:** $\{e_x, e_y, e_z\}$, where $e_y \equiv B$ (direction of motion), $e_x$ is perpendicular to $e_y$ in the plane spanned by $e_{\hat{r}}$ and $e_{\hat{\phi}}$, and $e_z$ completes the right-handed system.
* **Local Sky Coordinates:** Spherical polar angles $(\theta_{cs}, \phi_{cs})$ defined with respect to the camera’s Cartesian basis.

### Geodesic Constants of Motion
For any null geodesic, the covariant coordinate components of its 4-momentum $p_\alpha$ are governed by:
* Conserved energy at infinity: $-p_t$ (conventionally scaled to $1$).
* Conserved axial angular momentum: $b = -p_\phi / p_t$.
* Conserved Carter constant: $q = \text{Carter Constant} / p_t^2$.

### Ray-Bundle Geodesic Deviation Quantities (Directly Usable)
To compute the elliptical deformation of a circular pixel beam of initial diameter $\delta_{cs}$, the paper tracks five real quantities along the central ray as functions of the affine parameter $\zeta$:
* **Complex Deviation Variables:** $\xi(\zeta) = u(\zeta) + iv(\zeta)$ and $\eta(\zeta) = g(\zeta) + ih(\zeta)$.
* **Parallel Transport Angle:** $\chi(\zeta)$ (tracks the rotation of the beam's axes relative to the coordinate basis).
* **Major and Minor Angular Diameters on Source:** $\delta_+$ and $\delta_-$.
* **Ellipse Orientation Angle:** $\mu$ (relative to the celestial sphere’s $e_{\theta'}$ direction).

## 4. Methods / Algorithms

### 1. Ray-Tracing Pipeline (Backward Integration)
* **Step 1: Lens Initialization.** Select pixel coordinate $(\theta_{cs}, \phi_{cs})$ on the camera's local sky. Compute the incoming ray unit vector $N$ in the camera frame:
  $$N_x = \sin\theta_{cs} \cos\phi_{cs}, \quad N_y = \cos\theta_{cs}, \quad N_z = \sin\theta_{cs} \sin\phi_{cs}$$
* **Step 2: Relativistic Aberration.** Transform $N$ to the FIDO frame to obtain the FIDO-frame incoming ray direction $n_F$:
  $$n_{Fx} = \frac{\sqrt{1-\beta^2} N_x}{1 - \beta N_y}, \quad n_{Fy} = \frac{N_y - \beta}{1 - \beta N_y}, \quad n_{Fz} = \frac{\sqrt{1-\beta^2} N_z}{1 - \beta N_y}$$
* **Step 3: Initial Momenta.** Project $n_F$ onto $\{e_{\hat{r}}, e_{\hat{\theta}}, e_{\hat{\phi}}\}$ to determine the starting canonical momenta $p_r, p_\theta, p_\phi$, setting $p_t = -1$.
* **Step 4: Classification of Origin.** Check if the constants of motion $\{b, q\}$ lie within the unstable photon orbit trapping region $q_o(b_o)$ defined parameterised by $r_o \in [r_1, r_2]$. If yes, check the sign of the radial momentum $p_r$. If no, find the largest real root of the radial coordinate equation $R(r) = 0$. If $r_c \ge r_{up}$ (upper turning point), the ray originates from the celestial sphere; otherwise, it originates from the horizon.
* **Step 5: Geodesic Integration.** Integrate the first-order super-Hamiltonian ray equations backward in time:
  $$\frac{dx^\alpha}{d\zeta} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial p_\alpha}, \quad \frac{dp_\alpha}{d\zeta} = -\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial x^\alpha}$$
  Stop when the ray hits the horizon or reaches the celestial sphere ($r = \infty$ at $\zeta = \zeta_f$), yielding the source coordinate $(\theta', \phi')$.

### 2. Ray-Bundle Geodesic Deviation Integration
* Couple the 5 differential equations for $u, v, g, h, \chi$ to the ray-tracing integrator.
* **Boundary Conditions at Camera ($\zeta=0$):**
  $$u = 1, \quad v = 0, \quad g = 0, \quad h = 0, \quad \dot{u} = \dot{v} = \dot{g} = \dot{h} = 0, \quad \chi = 0$$
* Integrate backward along the reference ray to the source ($\zeta = \zeta_f$). Compute the major and minor angular diameters at the source:
  $$\delta_+ = \delta' \left( |\xi| + |\eta| \right), \quad \delta_- = \delta' \left| |\xi| - |\eta| \right|$$
  where $\delta' = \delta_{cs} (f_c / f_{F})$ evaluates the unlensed angular scale.
* Calculate the orientation of the major axis:
  $$\mu = \chi + \frac{1}{2} \arg(\xi \eta)$$

### 3. Spatial and Temporal Filtering
* **Spatial Anti-Aliasing:** Set the initial beam diameter $\delta_{cs}$ to *twice the pixel separation*. Modulate the collected light using a truncated Gaussian filter over the calculated ellipse.
* **Analytic Motion Blur:** Calculate the derivatives of the geodesic equations with respect to camera motion using automatic differentiation. The beam forms a "swept ellipse" over the shutter open time $\Delta T$, which is integrated analytically rather than via stochastic time-sampling.

## 5. Results & Key Parameters
* **Near-Extremal Spin Effects ($a = 0.999$):**
  * **Primary Caustic:** A tiny astroid curve on the celestial sphere, spanning only $0.45^\circ$ in latitude and longitude.
  * **Secondary Caustic:** Large, highly stretched astroid wrapping up to $263^\circ$ in longitude (for a camera at $r_c = 6.03M$) and a full $360^\circ$ circuit (for $r_c = 2.60M$).
  * **Tertiary Caustic:** Extremely elongated by frame-dragging; wraps more than six full circuits around the celestial sphere at $r_c = 2.60M$.
* **Image Multiplicity near the Shadow Edge:** Light rays temporarily trapped in prograde, spherical photon orbits create multiple images of the background scene. At $r_c = 2.60M$, this manifests as 3 distinct images of the galactic plane between the primary and secondary critical curves, and 8 images between the secondary and tertiary critical curves.
* **Relativistic Aberration:** A static camera ($d\phi/dt = 0$) at $r_c = 2.60M$ moves at a FIDO-relative speed of $-0.813c$. Special relativistic aberration causes the black hole shadow to expand, covering more than $2\pi$ steradians of the camera's sky.

## 6. Relevance to a Kerr + Accretion-Disk Offline Renderer
1. **Flicker-Free High-Resolution Rendering:** Evolving the deviation variables ($u, v, g, h, \chi$) allows the GPU renderer to sample thin features (like background stars or disk dust lanes) over a finite area without temporal or spatial aliasing.
2. **Volumetric Disk Mipmapping:** For a 3D voxel-based accretion disk, the local beam cross-section (governed by $\delta_+$ and $\delta_-$) directly dictates the correct mipmap level to sample. This prevents rendering artifacts in highly lensed, high-shear regions near the horizon.
3. **Relativistic Beaming and Temperature Shifts:** For a physical blackbody disk (e.g., $T = 4500\text{K}$), the renderer must scale the local specific intensity using the computed frequency shift $f_c/f'$:
  $$I_\nu = I_{\nu'} \left(\frac{f_c}{f'}\right)^3$$
  This produces correct Doppler-beaming, shifting the left (approaching) side of the disk to bright blue and the right (receding) side to a faint, dark red.
