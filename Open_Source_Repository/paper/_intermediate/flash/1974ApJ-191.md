# Page & Thorne (1974) Accretion Disk Model: Implementation Report

## 1. Identification
*   **Authors:** Don N. Page, Kip S. Thorne
*   **Year:** 1974 (Received December 26, 1973)
*   **Title:** Disk-Accretion Onto a Black Hole. I. Time-Averaged Structure of Accretion Disk
*   **Venue:** Astrophysical Journal (Ap. J., Vol. 191, pp. 499-506)

---

## 2. Core Contribution
The paper derives a general, model-independent, and explicit algebraic framework for the time-averaged radial structure of a thin, Keplerian accretion disk in a stationary, axisymmetric spacetime (specialized to the Kerr geometry). By employing conservation laws for rest mass, angular momentum, and energy, the authors show that the local time-averaged flux of radiant energy $F(r)$ emitted from the disk's surface can be determined analytically without specifying the microphysics of the internal disk viscosity (e.g., magnetic or turbulent stresses). The solution is uniquely determined by the black hole's mass $M$, spin $a$, the mass accretion rate $\dot{M}_0$, and a zero-torque boundary condition at the innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO, $r_{ms}$).

---

## 3. Key Equations & Quantities

### Coordinate System & Spacetime Metric
The analysis assumes a stationary, axisymmetric, reflection-symmetric metric. Near the equatorial plane ($z \ll r$), the metric is written in $(t, r, z, \phi)$ coordinates as:
$$ds^2 = -e^{2\nu} dt^2 + e^{2\psi}(d\phi - \omega dt)^2 + e^{2\mu} dr^2 + dz^2$$

### Units
The paper uses geometric units: $G = c = k_B = 1$. In these units, mass, length, and time are scaled to the black hole mass $M$ (i.e., $M = 1$). 

### Core Quantities & Kerr Spacetime Specializations
For a GPU ray tracer, the following algebraic quantities must be evaluated at the point of ray-disk intersection. We define the dimensionless helper variable $x \equiv \sqrt{r/M}$ and the dimensionless spin parameter $a_* \equiv a/M = 0.999$.

1.  **Angular Velocity ($\Omega$):** [**Directly Usable**]
    The angular velocity of circular geodesic orbits:
    $$\Omega = \frac{1}{M(x^3 + a_*)}$$

2.  **Specific Energy-at-Infinity ($E^\dagger$):** [**Directly Usable**]
    $$E^\dagger = \frac{x^2 - 2 + a_* x^{-1}}{x \left(x^2 - 3 + 2 a_* x^{-1}\right)^{1/2}}$$

3.  **Specific Angular Momentum-at-Infinity ($L^\dagger$):** [**Directly Usable**]
    $$L^\dagger = M x \frac{1 - 2 a_* x^{-3} + a_*^2 x^{-4}}{\left(x^2 - 3 + 2 a_* x^{-1}\right)^{1/2}}$$

4.  **Innermost Stable Circular Orbit ($r_{ms}$):** [**Directly Usable**]
    The radius of the inner edge of the disk. For $a_* = 0.999$, $r_{ms} \approx 1.182 M$ (prograde).

5.  **Time-Averaged Radiant Energy Flux ($F(r)$):** [**Directly Usable**]
    The flux of radiant energy flowing out of one face of the disk (measured in the local rest frame of the disk gas):
    $$F(r) = \frac{\dot{M}_0}{4 \pi r} f(r)$$
    where $\dot{M}_0$ is the constant accretion rate, and $f(r)$ is defined as:
    $$f(r) = -\frac{\Omega_{,r}}{\left(E^\dagger - \Omega L^\dagger\right)^2} \int_{r_{ms}}^{r} \left(E^\dagger - \Omega L^\dagger\right) L^\dagger_{,r} \, dr$$

6.  **Internal Torque ($W_\phi(r)$):**
    The time-averaged internal torque per unit circumference:
    $$W_\phi(r) = \dot{M}_0 \left(E^\dagger - \Omega L^\dagger\right)^{-1} f(r)$$

## 4. Methods / Algorithms

The radial structure equations are derived using the following steps:

1.  **Circular Geodesics:** Assume disk material moves in nearly geodesic equatorial orbits, establishing the thermodynamic identity:
    $$dE^\dagger = \Omega dL^\dagger$$
2.  **Conserved Flux Integration:** Apply Gauss's theorem to the differential conservation laws of rest-mass, angular momentum, and energy over a cylindrical control volume of height $2H$ and radial width $\Delta r$:
    *   **Rest Mass:** Yields a constant inward mass transport rate: $\dot{M}_0 = -2\pi \Sigma \sqrt{-g} u^r$.
    *   **Angular Momentum:** Equates the change in transported orbital angular momentum to the sum of internal torque $W_\phi$ and the angular momentum carried away by surface radiation.
    *   **Energy:** Equates the change in transported energy to the sum of work done by internal torques ($Q W_\phi$) and the energy lost to surface radiation $F(r)$.
3.  **Boundary Condition:** The integration constant is fixed by asserting that at the ISCO boundary ($r = r_{ms}$), the disk material becomes dynamically unstable and plunges rapidly into the black hole. This prevents any viscous torque from acting across the boundary, yielding the critical boundary condition:
    $$W_\phi(r_{ms}) = 0 \implies f(r_{ms}) = 0$$
4.  **Profile Integration:** Substituting this boundary condition into the integrated conservation laws yields the explicit radial integral for $f(r)$ shown in Section 3.

---

## 5. Results & Key Parameters
*   **Radiative Profile:** The main result is a closed-form radial profile of $F(r)$. The flux starts at $0$ at the inner boundary $r_{ms}$, rises rapidly to a sharp peak slightly outside $r_{ms}$, and asymptotically decays as $r^{-3}$ at large distances ($r \gg r_{ms}$).
*   **Key Parameters:**
    *   $\dot{M}_0$: Scales the overall radiation intensity linearly.
    *   $M$: Scales the spatial dimensions of the disk.
    *   $a_*$ (Spin): Dictates $r_{ms}$. For a near-extremal spin ($a_* = 0.999$), $r_{ms}$ is very close to the event horizon. This concentrates the bulk of the radiation in the deep gravitational potential well of the black hole, maximizing relativistic effects (gravitational redshift, light bending, and Doppler boosting).

## 6. Relevance to a Kerr + Accretion-Disk Offline Renderer

For an offline GPU ray tracer, this paper provides the physical foundation for shading the accretion disk:

1.  **Ray-Disk Intersection Shading:**
    When a traced geodesic camera ray intersects the equatorial plane ($z = 0$) at a radius $r \ge r_{ms}$, the renderer must compute the local emission properties of the disk:
    *   If $r < r_{ms}$, the local emission is set to zero (representing the non-radiating plunge region).
    *   If $r \ge r_{ms}$, the renderer calculates the local energy flux $F(r)$.
2.  **GPU Optimization (Lookup Tables):**
    Direct numerical integration of $f(r)$ on the fly in a GPU shader is computationally expensive. Because $f(r)$ depends only on the spatial coordinate $r$ and the constant spin $a_* = 0.999$, it should be precomputed as a 1D double-precision lookup table (LUT) or texture indexed by $x = \sqrt{r/M}$ and read during shader execution.
3.  **Temperature Mapping:**
    Assuming the disk is locally optically thick and emits as a blackbody, the local effective temperature is:
    $$T_{eff}(r) = \left(\frac{F(r)}{\sigma_{SB}}\right)^{1/4}$$
    The renderer uses $T_{eff}(r)$ to evaluate the Planck spectral intensity $B_\nu(T_{eff}(r))$ at the emission frequency.
4.  **Relativistic Redshift and Intensity Correction:**
    To transform the local emitted intensity to the observer frame, the renderer needs the disk's 4-velocity $u^\mu_{emit} = u^t(1, 0, 0, \Omega)$. The metric components and the Keplerian velocity $\Omega$ determine $u^t$ via normalization ($u^\mu u_\mu = -1$):
    $$u^t = \left(-g_{tt} - 2\Omega g_{t\phi} - \Omega^2 g_{\phi\phi}\right)^{-1/2}$$
    The redshift factor is computed from the ray's 4-momentum $p_\mu$ and the emission 4-velocity:
    $$g = \frac{(p_\mu u^\mu)_{obs}}{(p_\nu u^\nu)_{emit}}$$
    The observed specific intensity is then scaled by the relativistic invariant:
    $$I_{\nu, obs} = g^3 B_{\nu}\left(T_{eff}(r)\right)$$
