# Summary: Page & Thorne (1974) — Disk-Accretion onto a Black Hole. I.

## Identification

- **Authors:** Don N. Page, Kip S. Thorne
- **Title:** Disk-Accretion onto a Black Hole. I. Time-Averaged Structure of Accretion Disk
- **Journal:** *The Astrophysical Journal*, 191, 499–506 (1974)
- **ADS key:** 1974ApJ...191..499P

---

## Core Contribution

The paper derives an explicit, algebraic expression for the radial dependence of the **time-averaged energy flux F(r)** emitted from both faces of a geometrically thin, steady-state accretion disk surrounding any stationary, axially symmetric (and reflection-symmetric) black hole. The result holds for any metric of that symmetry class; the Kerr metric is treated as the primary application. The boundary condition used is zero torque at the innermost marginally stable circular orbit (ISCO), denoted r_ms.

---

## Key Equations & Quantities

**Coordinate system and units:** The general derivation uses a stationary, axisymmetric metric written in coordinates (t, r, z, φ), with the disk confined to the equatorial plane z = 0. The Kerr specialization uses Boyer-Lindquist coordinates (t, r, θ, φ). Units throughout are geometric: G = M = c = 1 (mass M of the black hole sets the length scale).

**Orbital quantities for circular equatorial geodesics (eq. 7a, 7b):**
- E\*(r) — specific energy at infinity of a circular orbit at radius r
- L†(r) — specific angular momentum of a circular orbit at radius r
- Ω(r) — angular velocity of circular geodesic orbits

These satisfy the thermodynamic-like relation (eq. 18):

> dE\* = Ω dL†

**Energy flux (eq. 11a/11b/11c combined):**

> F(r) = −(dM/dt) / (4π√−g) · Ω,r / (E\* − Ω L†)² · ∫[r_ms to r] (E\* − Ω L†) L†,r dr

where dM/dt is the (negative) rest-mass accretion rate, √−g is the metric determinant factor, and commas denote partial derivatives with respect to r. The integral function f(r) (eq. 12/34) encapsulates the cumulative angular-momentum transport from r_ms inward to r.

**Torque per unit circumference W_φ(r) (eq. 11b):** The local viscous torque. The boundary condition W_φ(r_ms) = 0 (no torque at inner edge) closes the system and determines the integration constant.

---

## Methods

1. Apply conservation of rest mass, angular momentum, and energy in the steady-state, thin-disk limit (H ≪ r; negligible specific heat).
2. Derive a general relation between the surface stress tensor component W_φ and the orbital integrals, valid for any axisymmetric metric.
3. Impose the zero-torque inner boundary condition at r_ms.
4. Specialize to the Kerr metric; substitute analytic expressions for E\*(r), L†(r), Ω(r), and the metric determinant. The result is an explicit algebraic-plus-integral formula requiring only quadrature over known functions of r and the spin parameter a.

---

## Results

- A closed-form expression for F(r) as a function of r, M, a, and the accretion rate dM/dt.
- The flux vanishes at r = r_ms (due to the zero-torque boundary) and at r → ∞.
- The peak of F(r) shifts inward and increases in magnitude as the spin parameter a increases toward the extremal value a = M.
- The framework yields the Novikov-Thorne steady-state disk models as a direct application.

## Relevance to a Kerr Black Hole + Accretion Disk GPU Renderer

- **Temperature profile:** F(r) is proportional to T(r)⁴ (Stefan-Boltzmann), directly setting the disk color temperature as a function of Boyer-Lindquist radius r. The renderer's simple model T = T₀·(6/r)^0.75 approximates this radial scaling; the full Page-Thorne F(r) formula can replace it for physical accuracy.
- **ISCO inner edge:** r_ms (ISCO) determines where the disk emission terminates. The renderer config parameter r_isco must match the Page-Thorne r_ms for the chosen spin a = 0.999.
- **Spin dependence:** Near-extremal spin (a = 0.999, as used in this project) maximizes F near the horizon; the renderer's emissivity kernel must account for this concentration.
- **Coordinate translation:** The paper uses Boyer-Lindquist coordinates for the Kerr specialization; the renderer operates in Cartesian Kerr-Schild (CKS). The BL radius r is identical in both coordinate systems (it is the CKS r), so F(r) applies directly once the CKS r-field is computed from x, y, z.
