## 2. Core Contribution

This paper makes no new technical contribution. It is a first-person historical account tracing how the visual appearance of black holes and their accretion disks was first computed and rendered. The author documents his own 1979 simulation (the first published image of a black hole with a thin accretion disk), contextualizes it within contemporaneous work (Bardeen 1973; Cunningham & Bardeen 1972/1973), and traces subsequent developments through colored images (Fukue & Yokoyama 1988; Marck 1989–1994), Kerr disk simulations (Viergutz 1993; Fanton et al. 1997), and the VLBI/EHT shadow feasibility program (Falcke et al. 2000). No formulas are derived or extended. All equations referenced are attributed to prior literature.

## 3. Key Equations & Quantities

**This paper is historical and qualitative. No new equations are derived or presented.** The following physical facts are stated in narrative form and traced to cited sources:

- **ISCO (Schwarzschild):** r = 6M (geometric units G = c = 1); used as the inner edge of the accretion disk in Luminet (1979) and all Schwarzschild-era simulations.
- **Extremal Kerr spin:** a = M (same geometric units); cited as the limiting case for which the shadow becomes "D-shaped" and strongly asymmetric.
- **Shadow apparent radius:** approximately 3√3/2 times the event horizon radius (stated qualitatively; attributed to Bardeen 1973 for the Schwarzschild case).
- **Disk emissivity model:** Shakura & Sunyaev (1973) for Newtonian/semi-relativistic disk structure; Page & Thorne (1974) for the fully relativistic time-averaged disk structure. The paper confirms the emissivity peaks near the ISCO and falls off outward.
- **Spectral shift:** Combined gravitational redshift and Doppler shift causes the approaching (left/blueshifted) side of the disk to appear significantly brighter than the receding side. This asymmetry is the most visually striking feature of all images described.
- **Coordinate system used in all historical simulations described:** Boyer-Lindquist (t, r, θ, φ). No Cartesian Kerr-Schild (CKS) work is mentioned anywhere in the paper.

All quoted numbers and relations are as stated by Luminet; none are independently derived here.

## 4. Methods / Algorithms

The paper describes rather than defines algorithms. Techniques mentioned across the reviewed works:

- **Isoradial curves:** Loci on the image plane corresponding to a fixed Boyer-Lindquist radius r on the disk; used in Luminet (1979) to map disk structure to the observer's image plane without full geodesic integration.
- **Isophotes:** Curves of constant apparent bolometric flux in the image plane; computed by combining isoradial maps with the disk emissivity profile and the combined gravitational + Doppler spectral shift.
- **Geodesic integration in Boyer-Lindquist coordinates:** Used by Bardeen (1973), Cunningham & Bardeen (1972/1973), Marck (1991/1994, Schwarzschild), Viergutz (1993, Kerr), and Fanton et al. (1997, Kerr). No specific integrator (RK4, Hamiltonian, etc.) is named; the paper is purely historical.
- **Transfer function approach:** Mentioned in the context of Cunningham & Bardeen (1972/1973); maps image-plane pixels to disk emission points via a precomputed function of Boyer-Lindquist coordinates.
- **Primary and secondary images:** Gravitational lensing produces a direct (primary) disk image and at least one additional (secondary) image looping around the black hole. Both are visible in all the described simulations.

## 5. Results & Key Parameters

All results are historical reconstructions of prior published images:

- **Luminet (1979):** First published image of a Schwarzschild black hole with a geometrically thin, optically thick accretion disk. Bolometric flux map; false-color temperature contours. Disk inner edge at r = 6M; asymmetric brightness due to combined redshift/blueshift; primary and secondary images visible.
- **Fukue & Yokoyama (1988):** Extended Luminet's approach to produce color images distinguishing thermal emission at different disk radii.
- **Marck (1989–1994):** Color animations for Schwarzschild spacetime; first motion-picture rendering of a black hole accretion system.
- **Viergutz (1993):** First systematic Kerr disk simulations. Images differ from Schwarzschild: ISCO moves inward for prograde orbits, shadow becomes asymmetric, Doppler asymmetry is more extreme at high spin.
- **Fanton et al. (1997):** Kerr ray-tracing code; monochromatic line profiles and images as a function of spin parameter.
- **Falcke, Melia & Agol (2000):** Demonstrated that the black hole shadow of Sgr A* is resolvable with VLBI baselines; set the observational agenda for EHT.

Key numerical parameters (all from cited prior literature):
- Schwarzschild ISCO: r = 6M
- Extremal Kerr spin: a = M
- Shadow apparent size: ~3√3/2 × r_horizon (Bardeen 1973)
