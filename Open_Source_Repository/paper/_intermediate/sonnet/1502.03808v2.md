# Paper Summary: 1502.03808v2

## 1. Identification

**Authors:** Oliver James, Eugénie von Tunzelmann, Paul Franklin (Double Negative Ltd., London), Kip S. Thorne (Caltech)
**Year:** 2015
**Title:** "Gravitational Lensing by Spinning Black Holes in Astrophysics, and in the Movie Interstellar"
**Venue:** Classical and Quantum Gravity 32 (2015) 065001

---

## 2. Core Contribution

Describes DNGR (Double Negative Gravitational Renderer), a production-grade CPU ray tracer used for the film *Interstellar*. Key innovations: (1) propagates full ray *bundles* (not individual rays) via a variant of the Sachs optical scalar / Pineault-Roeder geodesic-deviation equations, enabling physically correct magnification and anti-aliasing; (2) derives an analytic (non-Monte Carlo) motion-blur algorithm that augments the ray and ray-bundle equations with camera-velocity derivatives; (3) provides a complete, reproducible prescription — from pixel coordinates to Boyer-Lindquist (BL) celestial-sphere coordinates — for null geodesic integration in Kerr spacetime. The paper doubles as a pedagogical tour of caustics, critical curves, and lensing orders for near-extremal spin (a/M = 0.999), and for the moderate spin (a/M = 0.6) used in the film.

## 3. Key Equations & Quantities

**Coordinate system:** Boyer-Lindquist (BL) (t, r, θ, φ), M = G = c = 1. This paper uses BL throughout; it does NOT use Cartesian Kerr-Schild. Metric signature (−+++).

**Observers:**
- FIDO (Fiducial Observer = LNRO): world line orthogonal to constant-t slices; orthonormal spherical basis {e_r̂, e_θ̂, e_φ̂}.
- Camera: moves at speed β relative to FIDO; velocity direction B = (B_r̂, B_θ̂, B_φ̂) in FIDO frame; own orthonormal frame {e_x, e_y, e_z} with e_y ≡ B (camera's forward direction of motion).

**Camera-sky coordinates:** (θ_cs, φ_cs) — spherical angles on the camera's local sky.
**Celestial-sphere coordinates:** (θ', φ') — BL angular coordinates evaluated as r → ∞.

**Ray constants of motion** (DIRECTLY USABLE):
- b = axial angular momentum per unit energy = −p_φ / p_t (with convention p_t = −1)
- q = Carter constant (normalized by p_t²); equals (Carter constant) / p_t² in general

**Trapped-orbit boundary** (DIRECTLY USABLE): parametric curve q_o(b_o) traced by photon spherical orbit radius r_o ∈ [r_1, r_2], where r_1 and r_2 are the prograde/retrograde limits. Rays with {b, q} inside this curve come from the horizon; outside, from the celestial sphere (subject to the radial turning-point check).

**Radial potential:** R(r) defined via BL geodesic equations (MTW Eqs. 33.32 form, avoided in integration; super-Hamiltonian form used instead — see below). r_up = largest real root of R(r) = 0.

**Ray equations integrated** (Appendix A.1, step vi — DIRECTLY USABLE): super-Hamiltonian form (well-behaved at turning points, unlike dr/dζ = ±√R). Affine parameter ζ; backward integration from ζ = 0 (camera) to ζ_f (celestial sphere). Source point: θ' = θ(ζ_f), φ' = φ(ζ_f).

**Frequency blueshift** (Appendix A.1, step vii — DIRECTLY USABLE): f_c / f' = ratio of camera-measured frequency to source frequency on celestial sphere; derived from 4-momentum contraction with camera 4-velocity.

**Ray-bundle (beam) variables** (Appendix A.2): five real functions u(ζ), v(ζ), g(ζ), h(ζ), χ(ζ) evolved alongside the reference ray via coupled ODEs. Initial conditions at camera (ζ = 0) set from pixel angular radius δ_cs; terminal values at ζ_f give:
- δ_+ = major angular diameter of the bundle on the celestial sphere
- δ_- = minor angular diameter
- μ = angle from e_θ' to the bundle's major axis

Key intermediate quantities along the ray: M (phase-space function), Re(Ψ_0*) and Im(Ψ_0*) (components of Weyl tensor contracted on ray momentum), auxiliary scalars Q_1, Q_2, w, S. Evolution equations for {u, v, g, h, χ} are Pineault-Roeder variant modified so the denominator is (p̂_t − p̂_r) instead of (p̂_t + p̂_r), making them non-singular for ingoing bundles.

**Specific intensity law** (DIRECTLY USABLE): I_ν ∝ ν³ (Liouville; used for disk brightness scaling with Doppler + gravitational shifts).

**Disk blackbody shift:** Temperature shift applied to blackbody spectrum; convolved with film spectral sensitivity curves to produce R, G, B. Doppler + gravitational combined factor ≈ 1.5× blueward (prograde limb, v ≈ 0.55c), ≈ 0.4× redward (retrograde limb).

## 4. Methods / Algorithms

**Step-by-step ray-tracing prescription (Appendix A.1):**
1. Place camera at BL position (r_c, θ_c, φ_c) with velocity β relative to FIDO.
2. For each pixel (θ_cs, φ_cs), compute Cartesian components of incoming ray direction N in camera frame.
3. Apply special-relativistic aberration to obtain FIDO-frame ray direction n_F in Cartesian coordinates; project onto FIDO spherical basis {e_r̂, e_θ̂, e_φ̂}.
4. Compute canonical momenta p_α with p_t = −1; derive b and q.
5. Determine horizon vs. celestial-sphere origin: test whether {b, q} lies in the trapped region (conditions A.5–A.6 on the parametric curve q_o(b_o)) and check sign of p_r vs. radial turning points.
6. Integrate super-Hamiltonian null geodesic equations backward in ζ to obtain (θ', φ') at the celestial sphere.
7. Compute f_c / f' for intensity scaling.

**Ray-bundle integration (Appendix A.2):**
- Circular bundle at camera: initialize {u, v, g, h, χ} with prescribed conditions; integrate coupled ODEs (A.23) backward alongside ray; recover δ+, δ−, μ at celestial sphere.
- Elliptical bundle at camera: additionally specify major-axis direction {J_x, J_y, J_z} in camera frame, transform to FIDO frame via aberration; set modified initial conditions incorporating axis direction and axis ratio e; then proceed as circular case.

**Numerical integration:** Runge-Kutta-Fehlberg adaptive step-size (custom C++ implementation). Evolving bundle alongside ray triples compute time per step on average relative to ray-only integration.

**Spatial filtering (Appendix A.3.1):** Beam radius initialized to 2× pixel separation. Stars treated as point sources; light collected into every beam intersecting the star, weighted by a truncated Gaussian filter (max 2% intensity variation between on-pixel and inter-pixel star positions).

**Temporal filtering / motion blur (Appendix A.3.2–A.3.3):** Analytic method: camera-velocity derivatives are tracked through the ray and ray-bundle equations using automatic differentiation, yielding a swept ellipse at the celestial sphere. Integration over the swept ellipse produces motion blur at ≈2× single-frame cost vs. ≈4× for 4-sample Monte Carlo. Formal basis: Sung et al. [60] intensity integral I(x, y) = ∫ ∑_l g_l(ω, t) r(ω, t) L_l(ω, t) dω dt / ΔT; approximated by linear Taylor expansion of L in t about shutter midpoint.

**Accretion disk implementations (Appendix A.6):**
1. Thin disk: ray-bundle intersection detection; sample artist texture; attenuate by optical thickness.
2. Volumetric disk: Houdini-generated ~17M voxels; Extinction-Based Sampling with mipmap levels selected by beam major-axis length.
3. Procedural disk: DNGR embedded as plug-in in Mantra renderer; procedural textures at arbitrary resolution; slowest, used only for close-ups.

## 5. Results & Key Parameters

**Black hole parameters:** Near-extremal a/M = 0.999 for scientific figures; a/M = 0.6 for *Interstellar* (Gargantua) film shots (moderate spin chosen for visual clarity and fewer extreme-distortion regions).

**Accretion disk (pedagogical example):** Thin equatorial disk, T = 4500 K blackbody (inner radius r_in = 9.26M, outer radius r_out = 18.70M, matching *Interstellar* paint swatch).

**Lensing orders:** Order-n image = photon with n poloidal (θ) turning points. Primary (n = 0), secondary (n = 1), tertiary (n = 2), etc. Each successive order maps the full celestial sphere into a narrower band approaching the critical curve.

**Critical curves and caustics:** Primary/secondary/tertiary caustics on celestial sphere (astroid shapes for source near caustic, fold and cusp types); corresponding critical curves on camera sky. Image pairs created/annihilated at critical curve crossings.

**Trapped-orbit region:** For a/M = 0.999, prograde limit r_1 and retrograde limit r_2 define the parametric boundary in {b, q} space separating horizon-origin from sky-origin rays.

**Render infrastructure:** DNGR — 40,000 lines C++, Linux CPU render farm (1633 Dell M620 blades, 20 cores each, 156 GB RAM). Single IMAX frame (23M pixels): 30 min to several hours on 10 cores; longest renders for close-up accretion disk. Not GPU-accelerated.

**Comparison with GRay:** GRay uses individual rays on GPU for astrophysics throughput; DNGR uses ray bundles on CPU for film-quality smoothness. DNGR's bundle approach equivalent to ray differentials (Igehy 1999) but extended to curved spacetime.

## 6. Relevance to a Kerr + Accretion-Disk Offline Renderer

**Directly usable (BL coordinates):**
- Complete step-by-step prescription (Appendix A.1) for pixel → {b, q} → trapped-orbit test → super-Hamiltonian backward integration → (θ', φ') on celestial sphere. This is a reference implementation blueprint.
- Super-Hamiltonian null geodesic form is numerically superior to the ±√R form at radial turning points — adopt for the Taichi integrator.
- Blueshift formula f_c/f' (step vii) directly gives the frequency ratio needed for I_ν ∝ ν³ disk intensity scaling.
- Trapped-orbit boundary algorithm (step v, conditions on q_o(b_o) and R(r) roots) is the correct horizon-shadow test.

**Adaptable (coordinate translation required):**
- The project's active renderer uses Cartesian Kerr-Schild (CKS), not BL. The ray equations and conserved quantities {b, q} are BL-native; porting to CKS requires the BL↔CKS Jacobian documented in SKILL.md.
- Ray-bundle / geodesic-deviation ODEs (Appendix A.2) are written in FIDO spherical frame (BL). The five integration variables {u, v, g, h, χ} and the Weyl tensor component Ψ_0* would need re-expression in the CKS tetrad if the bundle propagation is to be implemented GPU-side.
- Pineault-Roeder modification (denominator (p̂_t − p̂_r) not (p̂_t + p̂_r)) is critical for numerical stability and must be preserved.

**Algorithm decisions for GPU renderer:**
- The analytic motion-blur technique (§A.3.2) scales well and is preferable to Monte Carlo temporal sampling; its automatic-differentiation approach is GPU-implementable.
- Thin-disk intersection + optical-thickness attenuation (§A.6) is the simplest disk model and suitable for a first GPU pass.
- Spatial filtering via beam radius = 2× pixel separation with truncated Gaussian weighting prevents star-image stretching near critical curves — adopt for the Taichi star-map sampler.
- The paper's CPU architecture (farm-based, per-pixel independent) maps cleanly onto a Taichi GPU kernel where each pixel/thread is independent.
